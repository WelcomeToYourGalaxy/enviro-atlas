Scope & coverage
Every small marker is a legal instrument or doctrine — a statute, treaty, regulation, decree, court ruling, or a legal doctrine / norm. Markers sit where each instrument was adopted, enacted, or adjudicated: solid markers are in force or still controlling today, dashed markers are proposed or pending, not yet in force.

The large markers are the actors behind the instruments. The larger the marker or node, the greater the actor’s revenue, reach, or influence. Click any actor to light up its ties and see the laws it drives.

These 160 instruments are the keystones — the ones that set the pattern. Behind them sits the full law atlas — 213,000+ environmental and environment-related instruments drawn from FAOLEX and Climate Change Laws of the World, searchable from the panel in the bottom-right corner. It is close to complete for international treaties and national legislation, holds some subnational law (where the source recorded a state or province), and is sparse on local and municipal law, which neither database systematically collects. What's includedThe complete set of load-bearing international instruments (47) and landmark international rulings and arbitral awards (7), plus the load-bearing national instruments (86) and landmark national rulings (13), and a set of foundational legal doctrines & mechanisms (7) — shareholder primacy, limited liability, the rule of capture, corporate personhood, forum non conveniens, regulatory takings, and stabilization clauses — that bind through the courts and contracts rather than by statute. Together these are the framework treaties, enabling statutes, apex judgments, and background doctrines that set the terms the rest of environmental law follows. What's excludedSecond-order instruments that borrow their force from the keystones; echo-laws — further countries whose statutes simply copy a mechanism already shown; sub-national variants (individual U.S. state versions, provincial rules); and non-legal actors — companies, agencies, programmes, and infrastructure. Four further classes are held out for sourcing reasons, not lack of importance: non-English-only law (Chinese, Russian, Portuguese, Bahasa, French, Arabic) — confirmable only against English sources under the no-fabrication rule, so what can't be verified is omitted rather than guessed; sub-national / provincial instruments — outside the national-and-international scope, and too numerous to add without changing the load-bearing picture; confidential or long-tail ISDS awards — sealed, unpublished, or too obscure to source individually; and very recent 2026 instruments. How whole is this census?
By raw count<0.1%
ECOLEX indexes 100,000+ environmental legislation records plus thousands of treaties and court decisions, and climate-law databases track thousands more. By number, this map is a rounding error.
By reach / influencemost of the world
A handful of framework treaties, the principal national mining, petroleum, and forest laws, the bedrock protective statutes, and a short line of apex rulings set the terms almost every other instrument operates within.
By kind~90%
Nearly every kind of binding instrument the field runs on is present — the framework treaty, the conservation and pollution-control statute, the carbon market, the mining and petroleum code, the permitting and deregulation statute, the defender-suppression law, the rights-affirming ruling and the apex rollback, and the background doctrine.
By stance, among the keystones~28% protective · ~72% destructive · ~2% double-edged
Of the 160 instruments and doctrines, 45 are protective in purpose and 115 license, subsidise, shield, or suppress. Flip the Stance filter and count it for yourself: for every instrument written to defend the living world, roughly two to three are written to enable its use. A handful are genuinely double-edged.
By actor attribution~66%
Of the 160 instruments and doctrines, 105 are now tied to at least one documented actor — a government ministry or state company, a financier, an industry lobby, or a court — that writes, funds, upholds, or (for the protective laws) resists them (91 actors in all). The roughly 55 still without a named actor are mostly the remaining protective treaties and rulings and the cross-cutting doctrines, where resistance is real but genuinely diffuse — carried by blocs of states, whole industries, or the consensus rule itself rather than any single body. Sources: ECOLEX · Climate Change Laws of the World

Stance — what the instrument is for
Protective instruments are written to defend the environment — framework treaties, conservation statutes, and rights-affirming cases — even where they have since been narrowed, delayed, or diluted. Destructive / enabling instruments license, subsidise, or shield the harm, or suppress those who resist it. Markers look identical on the map; this filter is the only thing that separates them — so you can see the balance for yourself.
